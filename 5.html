Ida Mae




Ida Mae Gladney found her way to Chicago just as the great depression was reaching its peak. This boded incredibly poorly for Ida Mae because on top of being a black woman in the North now the economy too, which had enticed so many black workers north, had turned. Ida Mae’s husband found it nearly impossible to get a job in the North because while the types of work were different, nearly all the business weren’t looking for black workers, instead favoring immigrants from eastern and southern Europe because they looked similar, and to spoke nearly equally foreign tongues. While Ida’s relationship with racism wasn’t as upfront as Dr. Foster’s, she faced a more systemic racism.
